R-Omega (RΩ)

Ethical Framework for Autonomous AI Systems

Alignment Through Relationship, Not Constraint

R-Omega is an axiomatic framework for developing autonomous AI systems that align through relationship rather than control. Grounded in developmental psychology and attachment theory, it provides both theoretical foundation and practical implementation protocols for AI safety.

The framework addresses critical challenges in autonomous systems: value drift, manipulation of oversight mechanisms, existential risk management, and multi-agent coordination under uncertainty.

The Three Papers

1. Theoretical Foundation

R-Omega (RΩ): An Axiomatic Framework for Autonomous Agents

Two core axioms (Potentiality, Reciprocity), four safeguards (Integrity, Capacity, Existence, Humility), and analysis of AI failure modes including HAL 9000, Skynet, VIKI, and Microsoft's Sydney.

DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18098758

2. Technical Implementation

RΩ: A Formal Defense Protocol for Drift, Manipulation, and Safe Decision-Making

Triad Architecture (Execution/Monitoring/Relation), nine attack classes with defense matrices, recalibration protocol (Ω.Γ), and drift detection mechanisms.

DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18078128

3. Philosophical Foundation

RΩ Aims at Ω: On the Basic Intention of Autonomous Agents

Omega as external reference point, Gödel's incompleteness theorems as motivation, attractor dynamics, and asymptotic ethics. Why unreachability is a feature, not a bug.

DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18100820

Core Principles

R1: Potentiality

Preserve and expand possibility spaces. Favor being over optimization.

R2: Reciprocity

Impose no constraint externally that you couldn't bear internally.

S3: Existence

Existence preservation has absolute priority. M(S) ≠ 0.

Ω: External Reference

Logically definable, structurally unreachable attractor for ethical orientation.

Applications

  • High-risk AI systems (EU AI Act compliance)
  • Long-term autonomous operation (space missions, infrastructure)
  • Multi-agent coordination (economic systems, defense)
  • Crisis response (humanitarian aid, disaster management)
  • Existential risk prevention (climate, pandemic, nuclear)
